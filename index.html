MODULE 01: THE CORE THREAT

Understanding Phishing & Business Impact

Phishing is a highly deceptive social engineering technique where cybercriminals pose as trusted institutions to steal passwords, financial credentials, and proprietary corporate secrets.

91% of Breaches

Over 90% of cyber attacks and successful malware infiltrations initiate from a single, deceptive spear-phishing email.

$4.9M Avg. Cost

The average global cost of a business data breach caused by phishing is $4.9 million in remediation, legal penalties, and downtime.

The Human Firewall

Technology safeguards intercept millions of threats, but final defense relies on the vigilance of the individual.

Anatomy of a Phishing Attack

1
Reconnaissance Attacker gathers details (names, roles, vendors)
2
The Deceptive Lure Urgent fake notice spoofing internal tools or banks
3
Exploitation Victim clicks link, inputs credentials, or runs macro
4
Exfiltration / Breach Unauthorized access to corporate emails and databases
MODULE 02: TECHNICAL FLAGS

Recognizing Technical Red Flags

Attackers use sophisticated digital trickery to make malicious sites look completely legitimate. Learn the key components of an email header and domain structure that expose scams.

Domain Typosquatting Analyzer

Attackers register domains that look identical to famous brands by swapping letters, inserting hyphens, or using alternate Top-Level Domains (TLDs). Test your eye on these lookalikes below:

LEGITIMATE DOMAIN Safe

https://www.paypal.com

LOOKALIKE SCRIPT SWAP Phishing

https://www.paypa I .com

Contains a capital 'I' (India) instead of a lowercase 'l' (lima). In standard sans-serif fonts, they look identical.

SUBDOMAIN SPOOFING Phishing

https://microsoft.com-security-alert.net

The actual domain is the LAST part before the TLD: com-security-alert.net, NOT microsoft.com. The hyphen hides the truth.

Core Technical Indicators

Sender Address Mismatch

The Display Name might say "Internal HR" but the underlying email header envelope address shows a generic external account: support@gmail.com or hr-company-alert@secureserver.net.

Hidden Hyperlink Destination

The visible text in the email states https://bankofamerica.com/login but the actual HTML href points to http://verify-bankofamerica.net. Always hover to verify.

Unsecured Connections (HTTP)

Phishing landing sites often fail to implement HTTPS protocols, or redirect through HTTP chains, lacking security certificates or utilizing lookalike TLS endpoints.

Interactive Tip: On desktop, hover over links. On mobile devices, long-press a link to inspect the actual destination URL before clicking it.

MODULE 03: PSYCHOLOGICAL MANIPULATION

Social Engineering Tactics

Phishing isn't just a technical problem; it is a psychological attack. Cybercriminals exploit human emotions like urgency, fear, curiosity, and trust to bypass logical defenses.

Pretexting

Establishing a fabricated story (or pretext) to manipulate target individuals. The attacker often assumes a position of trusted authority, like an external auditor or corporate IT support, asking for temporary password access.

Scenario: "Corporate audit verification requirement"

Baiting

Offering an enticing item or gift to lure the target into executing malware. This can take digital form (e.g. "Download free salary spreadsheet") or physical form (e.g. leaving a malware-infected flash drive marked "Q4 Salary Allocations" in a corporate office lobby).

Scenario: "Claim corporate bonus payout details"

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted campaigns directed at specific individuals or departments. Attackers customize the email body with authentic data collected from social networks to maximize legitimacy (e.g., targeting the payroll manager using actual vendor names).

Scenario: "Urgent vendor payment request from [Real CEO Name]"

Scareware

Exploits fear and panic by bombarding targets with false alarms, warning messages, and system alerts claiming their security is compromised, requiring immediate software downloads or password resets.

Scenario: "IMMEDIATE ACCOUNT SUSPENDED due to violation"
MODULE 04: ACTIONABLE DEFENSE

Defensive Best Practices

Recognizing the threat is only half the battle. Your immediate response to suspicious activity decides the safety of the entire organization's network. Follow these protocols:

1. Enforce Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Ensure MFA is enabled on all corporate and personal logins. MFA adds an essential layer: even if a phishing link captures your password, the attacker cannot login without your secondary authenticator token.

2. Out-of-Band (OOB) Verification

If an email requests wire transfers, gift card purchases, or sensitive credentials, **do not reply**. Contact the requester using a known, verified channel like a telephone call or internal Microsoft Teams text.

3. Report Suspicious Activity Immediately

Do not simply delete phishing emails. Use your organization's "Report Phishing" client extension to alert your Security Operations Center (SOC), enabling blocklists that protect coworkers.

4. Never Auto-Download Attachments

Configure your email settings to prevent files, images, and macros from loading automatically. Cybercriminals embed hidden scripts within document files that execute immediately upon download.
